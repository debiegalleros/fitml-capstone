The problem

Online clothing returns run at roughly 35%, versus 8% in physical stores — costing retailers over $100 billion annually. Sizing uncertainty drives most of this, though shoppers also hesitate simply because they can't see how a garment will look on them before buying.

How sizing works

Enter your measurements once — height, weight (optional), bust, waist, hip, body type. FitML matches you to a size using the garment's size chart, then verifies that match with a model trained on real fit feedback from past purchases.

  • Several modeling approaches were tested; the best-performing one is in use.
  • When a recommendation sits near a size boundary, FitML flags it — lower confidence, brief explanation of the tradeoff — rather than guessing.

How the try-on works

Upload a photo. Full-body works best; half-body is fine for tops and dresses. FitML detects your pose and overlays the garment, scaled to your proportions and chosen size.

  • Your photo stays otherwise unchanged — background, pose, face all preserved.
  • This is a 2D preview, not a 3D simulation, but enough to show how a size or color will look on you.

Why fairness matters here

Most sizing tools are a black box — a recommendation with no way to check it's reliable across body types. FitML's model was tested directly against that standard, and audited for the reasoning behind its predictions. This surfaced a real performance gap for certain groups, which was corrected and verified with before-and-after results.

Privacy

Your photo is used only to pose the try-on preview — it never feeds the sizing model. Uploads are stored for at most 24 hours and then automatically deleted, face-blurring is on by default, and one session's photos can't be retrieved by another. This follows the proportionality, transparency, and retention-limit principles of the Philippines' Data Privacy Act of 2012 (RA 10173).
